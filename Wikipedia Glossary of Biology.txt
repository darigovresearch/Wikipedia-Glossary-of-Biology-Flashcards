"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiotic_component"" title=""Abiotic component"">abiotic component</a>"|"
Any non-living chemical or physical part of the environment that affects living <a href=""#organism""><span title=""See entry on this page at § organism"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">organisms</span></a> and the functioning of <a href=""#ecosystem""><span title=""See entry on this page at § ecosystem"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">ecosystems</span></a>, such as the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Earth&#39;s atmosphere"">atmosphere</a> and water resources.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abscission"" title=""Abscission"">abscission</a>"|"
The shedding of flowers, leaves, and/or fruit following formation of scar tissue in a plant.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_(skin)"" title=""Absorption (skin)"">absorption</a>"|"
A process in which one substance permeates another. A <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid"" title=""Fluid"">fluid</a> permeates or is dissolved by a liquid or solid. Skin absorption is a route by which substances can enter the body through the skin.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_spectrum"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Absorption spectrum"">absorption spectrum</a>"|"
The spectrum of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation"" title=""Electromagnetic radiation"">electromagnetic radiation</a> that has passed through a medium which absorbs radiation of certain <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength"" title=""Wavelength"">wavelengths</a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acclimatization"" title=""Acclimatization"">acclimatization</a>"|"
<a href=""#adaptation""><span title=""See entry on this page at § adaptation"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">Adaptation</span></a> to a new <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate"" title=""Climate"">climate</a>, as with a new temperature or altitude or environment.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetyl-CoA"" title=""Acetyl-CoA"">acetyl-CoA</a>"|"
A molecule that participates in many <a href=""#biochemistry""><span title=""See entry on this page at § biochemistry"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">biochemical</span></a> reactions in <a href=""#protein""><span title=""See entry on this page at § protein"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">protein</span></a>, <a href=""#carbohydrate""><span title=""See entry on this page at § carbohydrate"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">carbohydrate</span></a>, and <a href=""#lipid""><span title=""See entry on this page at § lipid"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">lipid</span></a> <a href=""#metabolism""><span title=""See entry on this page at § metabolism"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">metabolism</span></a>, notably the <a href=""#citric_acid_cycle""><span title=""See entry on this page at § citric acid cycle"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">citric acid cycle</span></a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acoelomate"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Acoelomate"">acoelomate</a>"|"
A type of <a href=""#animal""><span title=""See entry on this page at § animal"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">animal</span></a>, such as a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flatworm"" title=""Flatworm"">flatworm</a> or a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jellyfish"" title=""Jellyfish"">jellyfish</a>, with a body plan that lacks a fluid-filled <a href=""#coelom""><span title=""See entry on this page at § coelom"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">cavity</span></a> between the body wall and the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digestive_tract"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Digestive tract"">digestive tract</a>. Rather, semi-solid mesodermal tissues between the gut and body wall hold the animal's <a href=""#organ""><span title=""See entry on this page at § organ"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">organs</span></a> in place. Contrast <a href=""#coelomate""><span title=""See entry on this page at § coelomate"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">coelomate</span></a> and <a href=""#pseudocoelomate""><span title=""See entry on this page at § pseudocoelomate"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">pseudocoelomate</span></a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_potential"" title=""Action potential"">action potential</a>"|"
The local change in <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltage"" title=""Voltage"">voltage</a> that occurs when the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Membrane_potential"" title=""Membrane potential"">membrane potential</a> of a specific location along the <a href=""#cell_membrane""><span title=""See entry on this page at § cell membrane"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">membrane</span></a> of a <a href=""#cell""><span title=""See entry on this page at § cell"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">cell</span></a> rapidly depolarizes, such as when a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nerve_impulse"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Nerve impulse"">nerve impulse</a> is transmitted between <a href=""#neuron""><span title=""See entry on this page at § neuron"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">neurons</span></a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Activation_energy"" title=""Activation energy"">activation energy</a>"|"
The <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy"" title=""Energy"">energy</a> that an atomic system must acquire before a process (such as an emission or reaction) can occur.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_site"" title=""Active site"">active site</a>"|"
The part of an <a href=""#enzyme""><span title=""See entry on this page at § enzyme"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">enzyme</span></a> or <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antibody"" title=""Antibody"">antibody</a> at which <a href=""#substrate""><span title=""See entry on this page at § substrate"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">substrate</span></a> molecules bind and undergo a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reaction"" title=""Chemical reaction"">chemical reaction</a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_transport"" title=""Active transport"">active transport</a>"|"
Transport of a substance (such as a protein or <a href=""#drug""><span title=""See entry on this page at § drug"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">drug</span></a>) across a <a href=""#membrane""><span title=""See entry on this page at § membrane"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">cell membrane</span></a> against a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concentration_gradient"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Concentration gradient"">concentration gradient</a>. Unlike <a href=""#passive_transport""><span title=""See entry on this page at § passive transport"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">passive transport</span></a>, active transport requires an expenditure of energy.

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptive_radiation"" title=""Adaptive radiation"">adaptive radiation</a>"|"
The process by which organisms diversify rapidly from an ancestral <a href=""#species""><span title=""See entry on this page at § species"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">species</span></a> into a multitude of new forms, particularly when a change in the <a href=""#environment""><span title=""See entry on this page at § environment"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">environment</span></a> makes new resources available, creates new challenges, or opens new <a href=""#niche""><span title=""See entry on this page at § niche"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">niches</span></a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenine"" title=""Adenine"">adenine</a>"|"
A <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purine"" title=""Purine"">purine</a>-derived organic compound which is one of the four canonical <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleobase"" title=""Nucleobase"">nucleobases</a> used in the <a href=""#nucleic_acid""><span title=""See entry on this page at § nucleic acid"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">nucleic acids</span></a> <a href=""#dNA""><span title=""See entry on this page at § DNA"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">DNA</span></a> and <a href=""#rNA""><span title=""See entry on this page at § RNA"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">RNA</span></a>. Its derivatives are involved in a wide variety of <a href=""#biochemistry""><span title=""See entry on this page at § biochemistry"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">biochemical</span></a> reactions, including <a href=""#cellular_respiration""><span title=""See entry on this page at § cellular respiration"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">cellular respiration</span></a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate"" title=""Adenosine triphosphate"">adenosine triphosphate</a> (ATP)"|"
An organic compound derived from <a href=""#adenine""><span title=""See entry on this page at § adenine"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">adenine</span></a> that functions as the major source of energy for chemical reactions inside living <a href=""#cell""><span title=""See entry on this page at § cell"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">cells</span></a>. It is found in all forms of life and is often referred to as the ""molecular currency"" of intracellular energy transfer.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adipose_tissue"" title=""Adipose tissue"">adipose tissue</a>"|"
A type of loose <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connective_tissue"" title=""Connective tissue"">connective tissue</a> made of mostly <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adipocytes"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Adipocytes"">adipocytes</a> and found in human and animal <a href=""#tissue""><span title=""See entry on this page at § tissue"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">tissue</span></a>, where it is colloquially known as body fat.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerobic_organism"" title=""Aerobic organism"">aerobic</a>"|"
Capable of surviving and growing in the presence of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen"" title=""Oxygen"">oxygen</a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerobiology"" title=""Aerobiology"">aerobiology</a>"|"
The study of organic particles which are passively transported by the air, including <a href=""#bacteria""><span title=""See entry on this page at § bacteria"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">bacteria</span></a>, fungal spores, very small insects, pollen grains, and <a href=""#virus""><span title=""See entry on this page at § virus"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">viruses</span></a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture"" title=""Agriculture"">agriculture</a>"|"
The practice of cultivating land, growing food, and/or raising <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Livestock"" title=""Livestock"">livestock</a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agrobiology"" title=""Agrobiology"">agrobiology</a>"|"
The study of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_nutrition"" title=""Plant nutrition"">plant nutrition</a> and growth, especially as a way to increase <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crop_yield"" title=""Crop yield"">crop yield</a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alga"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Alga"">alga</a>"|"
<p class=""glossary-hatnote""><span role=""note"" class=""hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref""><i>(pl.)</i> <b>algae</b></span></p>
Any member of a diverse <a href=""#polyphyletic""><span title=""See entry on this page at § polyphyletic"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">polyphyletic</span></a> group of <a href=""#photosynthesis""><span title=""See entry on this page at § photosynthesis"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">photosynthetic</span></a>, <a href=""#eukaryote""><span title=""See entry on this page at § eukaryote"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">eukaryotic</span></a>, mostly aquatic organisms ranging from simple <a href=""#unicellular""><span title=""See entry on this page at § unicellular"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">unicellular</span></a> <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microalgae"" title=""Microalgae"">microalgae</a> to massive <a href=""#colony""><span title=""See entry on this page at § colony"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">colonial</span></a> or <a href=""#multicellular""><span title=""See entry on this page at § multicellular"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">multicellular</span></a> forms such as <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelp"" title=""Kelp"">kelp</a>. Algae may reproduce <a href=""#sexual_reproduction""><span title=""See entry on this page at § sexual reproduction"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">sexually</span></a> or <a href=""#asexual_reproduction""><span title=""See entry on this page at § asexual reproduction"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">asexually</span></a>, and are often compared to <a href=""#plant""><span title=""See entry on this page at § plant"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">plants</span></a>, though they lack most of the complex cell and tissue types that characterize true plants.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allopatric_speciation"" title=""Allopatric speciation"">allopatric speciation</a>"|"
A form of <a href=""#speciation""><span title=""See entry on this page at § speciation"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">speciation</span></a> which occurs when biological <a href=""#population""><span title=""See entry on this page at § population"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">populations</span></a> of the same <a href=""#species""><span title=""See entry on this page at § species"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">species</span></a> become isolated from each other to an extent that prevents or interferes with genetic interchange.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid"" title=""Amino acid"">amino acid</a>"|"
A class of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound"" title=""Organic compound"">organic compounds</a> containing an <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amine"" title=""Amine"">amine group</a> and a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboxylic_acid"" title=""Carboxylic acid"">carboxylic acid</a> group which function as the fundamental building blocks of <a href=""#protein""><span title=""See entry on this page at § protein"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">proteins</span></a> and play important roles in many other biochemical processes.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amniote"" title=""Amniote"">amniote</a>"|"
An organism which produces an <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egg"" title=""Egg"">egg</a> composed of a shell and membranes that creates a protected environment in which the <a href=""#embryo""><span title=""See entry on this page at § embryo"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">embryo</span></a> can develop out of water.

The branch of biology that studies the structure and <a href=""#morphology""><span title=""See entry on this page at § morphology"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">morphology</span></a> of living organisms and their various parts.

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal"" title=""Animal"">animal</a>"|"
Any member of a <a href=""#clade""><span title=""See entry on this page at § clade"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">clade</span></a> of <a href=""#multicellular""><span title=""See entry on this page at § multicellular"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">multicellular</span></a> <a href=""#eukaryote""><span title=""See entry on this page at § eukaryote"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">eukaryotic</span></a> organisms belonging to the <a href=""#kingdom""><span title=""See entry on this page at § kingdom"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">biological kingdom</span></a> <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animalia"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Animalia"">Animalia</a>. With few exceptions, animals <a href=""#heterotroph""><span title=""See entry on this page at § heterotroph"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">consume organic material</span></a>, <a href=""#aerobic""><span title=""See entry on this page at § aerobic"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">breathe oxygen</span></a>, are <a href=""#motile""><span title=""See entry on this page at § motile"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">able to move</span></a>, <a href=""#sexual_reproduction""><span title=""See entry on this page at § sexual reproduction"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">reproduce sexually</span></a>, and grow from a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blastula"" title=""Blastula"">blastula</a> during embryonic development. An estimated 7 million distinct animal <a href=""#species""><span title=""See entry on this page at § species"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">species</span></a> currently exist.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antibiotic"" title=""Antibiotic"">antibiotic</a>"|"
<p class=""glossary-hatnote""><span role=""note"" class=""hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref"">Also called an <b>antibacterial</b>.</span></p>
A type of antimicrobial <a href=""#drug""><span title=""See entry on this page at § drug"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">drug</span></a> used in the treatment and prevention of <a href=""#bacteria""><span title=""See entry on this page at § bacteria"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">bacterial</span></a> <a href=""#infection""><span title=""See entry on this page at § infection"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">infections</span></a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apoptosis"" title=""Apoptosis"">apoptosis</a>"|"

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selective_breeding"" title=""Selective breeding"">artificial selection</a>"|"
<p class=""glossary-hatnote""><span role=""note"" class=""hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref"">Also called <b>selective breeding</b>.</span></p>
The process by which humans use <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_breeding"" title=""Animal breeding"">animal breeding</a> and <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_breeding"" title=""Plant breeding"">plant breeding</a> to <a href=""#natural_selection""><span title=""See entry on this page at § natural selection"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">selectively</span></a> control the development of particular <a href=""#phenotype""><span title=""See entry on this page at § phenotype"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">phenotypic</span></a> <a href=""#phenotypic_trait""><span title=""See entry on this page at § phenotypic trait"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">traits</span></a> in organisms by choosing which individual organisms will <a href=""#reproduction""><span title=""See entry on this page at § reproduction"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">reproduce</span></a> and create <a href=""#offspring""><span title=""See entry on this page at § offspring"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">offspring</span></a>. While the deliberate exploitation of knowledge about <a href=""#genetics""><span title=""See entry on this page at § genetics"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">genetics</span></a> and <a href=""#reproductive_biology""><span title=""See entry on this page at § reproductive biology"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">reproductive biology</span></a> in the hope of producing desirable characteristics is widely practiced in <a href=""#agriculture""><span title=""See entry on this page at § agriculture"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">agriculture</span></a> and experimental biology, artificial selection may also be unintentional and may produce unintended (desirable or undesirable) results.

"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology"" title=""Astrobiology"">astrobiology</a>"|"
The branch of biology concerned with the effects of outer space on living organisms and the search for extraterrestrial life.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autoimmunity"" title=""Autoimmunity"">autoimmunity</a>"|"
The system of <a href=""#immunity""><span title=""See entry on this page at § immunity"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">immune</span></a> responses of an organism directed against its own healthy cells and tissues.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autotroph"" title=""Autotroph"">autotroph</a>"|"
<p class=""glossary-hatnote""><span role=""note"" class=""hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref"">Sometimes used interchangeably with <b><a href=""#primary_producer""><span title=""See entry on this page at § primary producer"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">primary producer</span></a></b>.</span></p>
An organism capable of producing complex <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound"" title=""Organic compound"">organic compounds</a> from simple substances present in its surroundings, generally by using energy from sunlight (as in <a href=""#photosynthesis""><span title=""See entry on this page at § photosynthesis"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">photosynthesis</span></a>) or from inorganic chemical reactions (as in <a href=""#chemosynthesis""><span title=""See entry on this page at § chemosynthesis"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">chemosynthesis</span></a>). Autotrophs do not need to consume another living organism in order to obtain energy or organic carbon, as opposed to <a href=""#heterotroph""><span title=""See entry on this page at § heterotroph"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">heterotrophs</span></a>.

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B_cell"" title=""B cell"">B cell</a>"|"
A type of lymphocyte in the humoral immunity of the adaptive immune system.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria"" title=""Bacteria"">bacteria</a>"|"
An enormous and diverse clade of microscopic, <a href=""#prokaryote""><span title=""See entry on this page at § prokaryote"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">prokaryotic</span></a>, single-celled organisms which lack a true <a href=""#nucleus""><span title=""See entry on this page at § nucleus"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">nucleus</span></a>. They represent one of the three fundamental biological <a href=""#domain""><span title=""See entry on this page at § domain"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">domains</span></a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteriophage"" title=""Bacteriophage"">bacteriophage</a>"|"
A <a href=""#virus""><span title=""See entry on this page at § virus"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">virus</span></a> that infects and multiplies within <a href=""#bacteria""><span title=""See entry on this page at § bacteria"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">bacteria</span></a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barr_body"" title=""Barr body"">Barr body</a>"|"
The inactive X chromosome in a female somatic cell, rendered inactive in a process called lyonization, in those species in which sex is determined by the presence of the Y (including humans) or W chromosome rather than the diploidy of the X or Z.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basal_body"" title=""Basal body"">basal body</a>"|"
An organelle formed from a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centriole"" title=""Centriole"">centriole</a>, and a short cylindrical array of microtubules. Also called a <i>basal granule</i>, a <i>kinetosome</i>, and in older cytological literature, a <i>blepharoplast</i>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Behavioral_ecology"" title=""Behavioral ecology"">behavioral ecology</a>"|"
The study of the evolutionary basis for animal behavior due to ecological pressures.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bile"" title=""Bile"">bile</a>"|"
A dark green to yellowish-brown fluid, produced by the liver of most vertebrates, which aids the digestion of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid"" title=""Lipid"">lipids</a> in the small intestine. Also called <i>gall</i>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fission_(biology)"" title=""Fission (biology)"">binary fission</a>"|"
The process by which one prokaryotic cell divides into two identical daughter cells.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature"" title=""Binomial nomenclature"">binomial nomenclature</a>"|"
A formal system of classifying <a href=""#species""><span title=""See entry on this page at § species"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">species</span></a> of living things by giving each a name composed of two parts, both of which use Latin grammatical forms, although they can be based on words from other languages.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biocatalysis"" title=""Biocatalysis"">biocatalysis</a>"|"
The process of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalysis"" title=""Catalysis"">catalysis</a> in biological systems. In biocatalytic processes, natural catalysts, such as protein enzymes, perform chemical transformations on organic compounds.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biochemistry"" title=""Biochemistry"">biochemistry</a>"|"
The branch of biology that studies the chemical properties, compositions, reactions, and processes related to living organisms.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity"" title=""Biodiversity"">biodiversity</a>"|"
A contraction of ""biological diversity"" generally referring to the variety and variability of life on Earth.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bioengineering"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Bioengineering"">bioengineering</a>"|"
The application of concepts and methods of biology to solve real-world problems related to the life sciences or the application thereof.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bioenergetics"" title=""Bioenergetics"">bioenergetics</a>"|"
The study of the transformation of energy in living organisms.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biogeography"" title=""Biogeography"">biogeography</a>"|"
The study of the distribution of species and ecosystems in geographic space and through geological time. Organisms and biological communities often vary in a regular fashion along geographic gradients of latitude, elevation, isolation and habitat area.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bioinformatics"" title=""Bioinformatics"">bioinformatics</a>"|"
The application of computer technology to the management of biological information.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_organization"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Biological organization"">biological organization</a>"|"
The hierarchy of complex biological structures and systems, designed to define life through a reductionist approach.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology"" title=""Biology"">biology</a>"|"
The study of life and living <a href=""#organism""><span title=""See entry on this page at § organism"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">organisms</span></a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomass"" title=""Biomass"">biomass</a>"|"
Organic matter derived from living or recently living organisms. Biomass can be used as a source of energy and it most often refers to plants or plant-based materials which are not used for food or feed, and are specifically called lignocellulosic biomass.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomathematics"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Biomathematics"">biomathematics</a>"|"
The theoretical use of mathematical models and abstractions of living systems to understand and predict biological problems.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biome"" title=""Biome"">biome</a>"|"
Any very large <a href=""#ecology""><span title=""See entry on this page at § ecology"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">ecological</span></a> area on the Earth's surface containing fauna and flora (animals and plants) adapting to their environment. Biomes are often defined by <a href=""#abiotic_component""><span title=""See entry on this page at § abiotic component"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">abiotic factors</span></a> such as climate, topographical relief, geology, soils, and water resources.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomechanics"" title=""Biomechanics"">biomechanics</a>"|"
The study of the structure and function of biological systems by means of the methods of ""mechanics"", which is the branch of physics involving analysis of the actions of forces.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomedical_engineering"" title=""Biomedical engineering"">biomedical engineering</a>"|"
The application of engineering principles and design concepts to medicine and biology for healthcare purposes (e.g. diagnostic or therapeutic).
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomedical_research"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Biomedical research"">biomedical research</a>"|"
The pursuit of answers to medical questions. These investigations lead to discoveries, which in turn lead to the development of new preventions, therapies, and cures for problems in human and veterinary health. Biomedical research generally takes two forms: basic science and applied research.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomolecule"" title=""Biomolecule"">biomolecule</a>"|"
<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecules"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Molecules"">Molecules</a> and <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ions"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Ions"">ions</a> that are present in organisms, essential to some typically biological process such as <a href=""#cell_division""><span title=""See entry on this page at § cell division"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">cell division</span></a>, <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphogenesis"" title=""Morphogenesis"">morphogenesis</a>, or development.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biophysics"" title=""Biophysics"">biophysics</a>"|"
The application of approaches traditionally employed in physics to study biological systems.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biotechnology"" title=""Biotechnology"">biotechnology</a>"|"
Biotechnology is the use of living systems and organisms to develop or make products, or ""any technological application that uses biological systems, living organisms or derivatives thereof, to make or modify products or processes for specific use"" (UN Convention on Biological Diversity).
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipedal"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Bipedal"">bipedal</a>"|"
A form of terrestrial locomotion where an organism moves by means of its two rear limbs or legs.

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-independent_reactions#Coupling_to_other_metabolic_pathways"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Light-independent reactions"">Calvin cycle</a>"|"
The Calvin cycle, light-independent reactions, bio synthetic phase, dark reactions, or photosynthetic carbon reduction (PCR) cycle<sup id=""cite_ref-1"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_biology#cite_note-1"">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis"" title=""Photosynthesis"">photosynthesis</a> are the chemical reactions that convert carbon dioxide and other compounds into <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glucose"" title=""Glucose"">glucose</a>. These reactions occur in the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stroma_(fluid)"" title=""Stroma (fluid)"">stroma</a>, the fluid-filled area of a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloroplast"" title=""Chloroplast"">chloroplast</a> outside the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thylakoid_membranes"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Thylakoid membranes"">thylakoid membranes</a>. These reactions take the products (<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate"" title=""Adenosine triphosphate"">ATP</a> and <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NADPH"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""NADPH"">NADPH</a>) of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-dependent_reactions"" title=""Light-dependent reactions"">light-dependent reactions</a> and perform further chemical processes on them. There are three phases to the light-independent reactions, collectively called the <i>Calvin cycle</i>: carbon fixation, reduction reactions, and ribulose 1,5-bisphosphate (RuBP) regeneration.
"

"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carotenoid"" title=""Carotenoid"">carotenoid</a>"|"
One of a class of organic pigments produced by <a href=""#algae""><span title=""See entry on this page at § algae"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">algae</span></a> and <a href=""#plant""><span title=""See entry on this page at § plant"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">plants</span></a>, as well as certain <a href=""#bacteria""><span title=""See entry on this page at § bacteria"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">bacteria</span></a> and <a href=""#fungi""><span title=""See entry on this page at § fungi"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">fungi</span></a>.

An <a href=""#enzyme""><span title=""See entry on this page at § enzyme"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">enzyme</span></a> found in nearly all living <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisms"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Organisms"">organisms</a> exposed to <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen"" title=""Oxygen"">oxygen</a>, including bacteria, plants, and animals.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology)"" title=""Cell (biology)"">cell</a>"|"
The basic structural and functional unit of all living <a href=""#organism""><span title=""See entry on this page at § organism"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">organisms</span></a>, and the smallest functional unit of life. A cell may exist as an independent, self-replicating unit (as in the case of <a href=""#unicellular""><span title=""See entry on this page at § unicellular"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">unicellular organisms</span></a>), or in cooperation with other cells, each of which may be specialized for carrying out particular functions within a larger <a href=""#multicellular""><span title=""See entry on this page at § multicellular"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">multicellular organism</span></a>. Cells consist of <a href=""#cytoplasm""><span title=""See entry on this page at § cytoplasm"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">cytoplasm</span></a> enclosed within a <a href=""#cell_membrane""><span title=""See entry on this page at § cell membrane"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">cell membrane</span></a> and sometimes a <a href=""#cell_wall""><span title=""See entry on this page at § cell wall"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">cell wall</span></a>, and serve the fundamental purpose of separating the controlled environment in which biochemical processes take place from the outside world. Most cells are visible only under a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microscope"" title=""Microscope"">microscope</a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_biology"" title=""Cell biology"">cell biology</a>"|"
<p class=""glossary-hatnote""><span role=""note"" class=""hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref"">Also called <b>cytology</b>.</span></p>
The branch of biology that studies the structure and function of living <a href=""#cell""><span title=""See entry on this page at § cell"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">cells</span></a>, including their <a href=""#physiology""><span title=""See entry on this page at § physiology"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">physiological</span></a> properties, <a href=""#metabolism""><span title=""See entry on this page at § metabolism"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">metabolic</span></a> processes, chemical composition, <a href=""#life_cycle""><span title=""See entry on this page at § life cycle"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">life cycle</span></a>, the <a href=""#organelle""><span title=""See entry on this page at § organelle"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">organelles</span></a> they contain, and their interactions with their environment. This is done at both <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microscope"" title=""Microscope"">microscopic</a> and <a href=""#molecular_biology""><span title=""See entry on this page at § molecular biology"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">molecular</span></a> levels.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_cycle"" title=""Cell cycle"">cell cycle</a>"|"
The ordered series of events which take place in a <a href=""#cell""><span title=""See entry on this page at § cell"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">cell</span></a> leading to <a href=""#dNA_replication""><span title=""See entry on this page at § DNA replication"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">duplication</span></a> of its genetic material and ultimately the <a href=""#cell_division""><span title=""See entry on this page at § cell division"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">division</span></a> of the <a href=""#cytoplasm""><span title=""See entry on this page at § cytoplasm"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">cytoplasm</span></a> and <a href=""#organelle""><span title=""See entry on this page at § organelle"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">organelles</span></a> to produce two or more daughter cells. These events can be broadly divided into phases of growth and division, each of which can vary in duration and complexity depending on the tissue or organism to which the cell belongs. Cell cycles are essential processes in all <a href=""#unicellular""><span title=""See entry on this page at § unicellular"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">unicellular</span></a> and <a href=""#multicellular""><span title=""See entry on this page at § multicellular"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">multicellular</span></a> organisms.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_division"" title=""Cell division"">cell division</a>"|"

Examples include <a href=""#binary_fission""><span title=""See entry on this page at § binary fission"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">binary fission</span></a>, <a href=""#mitosis""><span title=""See entry on this page at § mitosis"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">mitosis</span></a>, and <a href=""#meiosis""><span title=""See entry on this page at § meiosis"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">meiosis</span></a>.

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citric_acid_cycle"" title=""Citric acid cycle"">citric acid cycle</a>"|"
<p class=""glossary-hatnote""><span role=""note"" class=""hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref"">Also called the <b>Krebs cycle</b> and <b>tricarboxylic acid cycle (TCA)</b>.</span></p>
A series of chemical reactions used by all aerobic organisms to generate energy through the oxidation of acetyl-CoA derived from carbohydrates, fats and proteins into carbon dioxide and chemical energy in the form of guanosine triphosphate (GTP). In addition, the cycle provides precursors of certain amino acids as well as the reducing agent NADH that is used in numerous other biochemical reactions. Its central importance to many biochemical pathways suggests that it was one of the earliest established components of cellular metabolism and may have originated abiogenically.

A cytoskeleton is present in the <a href=""#cytoplasm""><span title=""See entry on this page at § cytoplasm"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">cytoplasm</span></a> of all <a href=""#cell""><span title=""See entry on this page at § cell"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">cells</span></a>, including <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria"" title=""Bacteria"">bacteria</a>, and <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaea"" title=""Archaea"">archaea</a>. "<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_efficiency"" title=""Ecological efficiency"">ecological efficiency</a>"|"
The efficiency with which energy is transferred from one <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trophic_level"" title=""Trophic level"">trophic level</a> to the next. It is determined by a combination of efficiencies relating to organismic resource acquisition and assimilation in an <a href=""#ecosystem""><span title=""See entry on this page at § ecosystem"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">ecosystem</span></a>. <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_production"" title=""Primary production"">Primary production</a> occurs in <a href=""#autotroph""><span title=""See entry on this page at § autotroph"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">autotrophic</span></a> organisms of an ecosystem.

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_pyramid"" title=""Ecological pyramid"">ecological pyramid</a>"|"
An ecological pyramid (also trophic pyramid, eltonian pyramid, energy pyramid, or sometimes food pyramid) is a graphical representation designed to show the biomass or bio productivity at each trophic level in a given ecosystem. Biomass is the amount of living or organic matter present in an organism.

It is an interdisciplinary field that includes biology, <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography"" title=""Geography"">geography</a>, and <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_science"" title=""Earth science"">Earth science</a>.

In evolutionary ecology, a genetically distinct geographic variety, <a href=""#population""><span title=""See entry on this page at § population"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">population</span></a>, or <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Race_(biology)"" title=""Race (biology)"">race</a> within a <a href=""#species""><span title=""See entry on this page at § species"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">species</span></a> which is adapted to specific environmental conditions.

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ectoderm"" title=""Ectoderm"">ectoderm</a>"|"
The outermost layer of cells or tissue of an <a href=""#embryo""><span title=""See entry on this page at § embryo"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">embryo</span></a> in early development, or the parts derived from this, which include the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epidermis"" title=""Epidermis"">epidermis</a>, nerve tissue, and <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nephridia"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Nephridia"">nephridia</a>.

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ectotherm"" title=""Ectotherm"">ectotherm</a>"|"
An organism in which internal physiological sources of heat are of relatively small or quite negligible importance in controlling <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Body_temperature"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Body temperature"">body temperature</a>. Colloquially, these organisms often referred to as ""cold-blooded"".

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effector_(biology)"" title=""Effector (biology)"">effector</a>"|"
A small molecule that selectively binds to a <a href=""#protein""><span title=""See entry on this page at § protein"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">protein</span></a> and regulates its <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_activity"" title=""Biological activity"">biological activity</a>. In this manner, effector molecules act as <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ligand_(biochemistry)"" title=""Ligand (biochemistry)"">ligands</a> that can increase or decrease <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allosteric_regulation"" title=""Allosteric regulation"">enzyme activity</a>, <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene_expression"" title=""Gene expression"">gene expression</a>, or <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_signaling"" title=""Cell signaling"">cell signaling</a>.

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrochemical_gradient"" title=""Electrochemical gradient"">electrochemical gradient</a>"|"
A gradient of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrochemical_potential"" title=""Electrochemical potential"">electrochemical potential</a>, usually for an <a href=""#ion""><span title=""See entry on this page at § ion"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">ion</span></a> that can move across a membrane. The gradient consists of two parts: the electrical potential and the difference in chemical concentration across the membrane.

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum"" title=""Electromagnetic spectrum"">electromagnetic spectrum</a>"|"
The range of all possible frequencies of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation"" title=""Electromagnetic radiation"">electromagnetic radiation</a>. When referring to an object, the electromagnetic spectrum is the characteristic distribution of electromagnetic radiation emitted or absorbed by that particular object.

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron"" title=""Electron"">electron</a>"|"
A negatively charged subatomic particle, symbolized by <i>e−</i> or <i>β−</i>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_acceptor"" title=""Electron acceptor"">electron acceptor</a>"|"
An electron acceptor is a chemical entity that accepts electrons transferred to it from another compound. It is an oxidizing agent that, by virtue of its accepting electrons, is itself reduced in the process.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_carrier"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Electron carrier"">electron carrier</a>"|"
electron carrier. Any of various molecules that are capable of accepting one or two electrons from one molecule and donating them to another in the process of electron transport. As the electrons are transferred from one electron carrier to another, their energy level decreases, and energy is released.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_donor"" title=""Electron donor"">electron donor</a>"|"
An electron donor is a chemical entity that donates electrons to another compound. It is a reducing agent that, by virtue of its donating electrons, is itself oxidized in the process.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_microscope"" title=""Electron microscope"">electron microscope</a>"|"
The electron microscope is a type of microscope that uses a beam of electrons to create an image of the specimen. It is capable of much higher magnifications and has a greater resolving power than a light microscope, allowing it to see much smaller objects in finer detail.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_transport_chain"" title=""Electron transport chain"">electron transport chain</a>"|"
The site in a mitochondrion of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidative_phosphorylation"" title=""Oxidative phosphorylation"">oxidative phosphorylation</a> in eukaryotes. The NADH and succinate generated by the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citric_acid_cycle"" title=""Citric acid cycle"">citric acid cycle</a> are oxidized, providing energy to power ATP synthase. Photosynthetic electron transport chain of the thylakoid membrane.

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid"" title=""Lipid"">lipid</a>"|"
A substance that is insoluble in water and soluble in alcohol, ether, and chloroform. Lipids are an important component of living cells. Together with carbohydrates and proteins, lipids are the main constituents of plant and animal cells. Cholesterol and triglycerides are lipids.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipoprotein"" title=""Lipoprotein"">lipoprotein</a>"|"
A biochemical assembly that contains both proteins and lipids, bound to the proteins, which allow fats to move through the water inside and outside cells. The proteins serve to emulsify the lipid molecules.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M_phase"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""M phase"">M phase</a>"|"
Mitosis and cytokinesis together define the mitotic (M) phase of an animal cell cycle – the division of the mother cell into two daughter cells, genetically identical to each other and to their parent cell.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macroevolution"" title=""Macroevolution"">macroevolution</a>"|"
Evolution on a scale of separated gene pools. Macroevolutionary studies focus on change that occurs at or above the level of species, in contrast with microevolution, which refers to smaller evolutionary changes (typically described as changes in allele frequencies) within a species or population.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macromolecule"" title=""Macromolecule"">macromolecule</a>"|"
A very large molecule, such as a protein, commonly created by polymerization of smaller subunits (monomers). They are typically composed of thousands or more atoms.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macronutrient"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Macronutrient"">macronutrient</a>"|"
Nutrients needed in large amounts which provide calories or energy. Nutrients are substances needed for growth, metabolism, and for other body functions. There are three basic types of macronutrients: fats, proteins, and carbohydrates.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macrophage"" title=""Macrophage"">macrophage</a>"|"
A kind of swallowing cell, which means it functions by literally swallowing up other particles or smaller cells. Macrophages engulf and digest debris (like dead cells) and foreign particles through the process of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phagocytosis"" title=""Phagocytosis"">phagocytosis</a>, so macrophages act like scavengers.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mammalogy"" title=""Mammalogy"">mammalogy</a>"|"
The study of mammals, a class of vertebrates with characteristics such as homeothermic metabolism, fur, four-chambered hearts, and complex nervous systems.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_biology"" title=""Marine biology"">marine biology</a>"|"
The study of organisms in the ocean or other marine bodies of water. Given that in biology many phyla, families and genera have some species that live in the sea and others that live on land, marine biology classifies species based on the environment rather than on taxonomy.
"

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitosis"" title=""Mitosis"">mitosis</a>"|"
In <a href=""#eukaryote""><span title=""See entry on this page at § eukaryote"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">eukaryotic</span></a> <a href=""#cell""><span title=""See entry on this page at § cell"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">cells</span></a>, the part of the <a href=""#cell_cycle""><span title=""See entry on this page at § cell cycle"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">cell cycle</span></a> during which the division of the <a href=""#nucleus""><span title=""See entry on this page at § nucleus"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">nucleus</span></a> takes place and duplicated <a href=""#chromosome""><span title=""See entry on this page at § chromosome"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">chromosomes</span></a> are separated into two distinct nuclei. Mitosis is generally preceded by the ""S"" stage of <a href=""#interphase""><span title=""See entry on this page at § interphase"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">interphase</span></a>, when the cell's <a href=""#dNA""><span title=""See entry on this page at § DNA"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">DNA</span></a> is <a href=""#dNA_replication""><span title=""See entry on this page at § DNA replication"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">replicated</span></a>, and followed by <a href=""#cytokinesis""><span title=""See entry on this page at § cytokinesis"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">cytokinesis</span></a>, when the <a href=""#cytoplasm""><span title=""See entry on this page at § cytoplasm"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">cytoplasm</span></a> and <a href=""#cell_membrane""><span title=""See entry on this page at § cell membrane"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">cell membrane</span></a> are divided into two new daughter cells. It is similar to but distinct from <a href=""#binary_fission""><span title=""See entry on this page at § binary fission"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">binary fission</span></a> and <a href=""#meiosis""><span title=""See entry on this page at § meiosis"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">meiosis</span></a>.

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule"" title=""Molecule"">molecule</a>"|"
The smallest particle in a chemical element or compound that has the chemical properties of that element or compound. Molecules are made up of atoms that are held together by chemical bonds. These bonds form as a result of the sharing or exchange of electrons among atoms.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_biology"" title=""Molecular biology"">molecular biology</a>"|"
The branch of biology concerning biological activity at the molecular level. The field of molecular biology overlaps with biology and chemistry and in particular with genetics and biochemistry.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_switch"" title=""Molecular switch"">molecular switch</a>"|"
 A molecule that can be reversibly changed between two or more stable states.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomer"" title=""Monomer"">monomer</a>"|"
A <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule"" title=""Molecule"">molecule</a> that ""can undergo polymerization thereby contributing constitutional units to the essential structure of a macromolecule"".

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motor_neuron"" title=""Motor neuron"">motor neuron</a>"|"
A neuron whose cell body is situated in the motor <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motor_cortex"" title=""Motor cortex"">cortex</a>, <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brainstem"" title=""Brainstem"">brainstem</a> or the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinal_cord"" title=""Spinal cord"">spinal cord</a>, and whose axon (fiber) projects to the spinal cord or outside of the spinal cord to directly or indirectly control effector organs, mainly muscles and glands.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mucous_membrane"" title=""Mucous membrane"">mucous membrane</a>"|"
A membrane that lines various cavities in the body and covers the surface of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_organs"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Internal organs"">internal organs</a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multicellular_organism"" title=""Multicellular organism"">multicellular</a>"|"
Having or consisting of more than one <a href=""#cell""><span title=""See entry on this page at § cell"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">cell</span></a>, as opposed to being <a href=""#unicellular""><span title=""See entry on this page at § unicellular"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">unicellular</span></a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mycology"" title=""Mycology"">mycology</a>"|"
The branch of biology concerned with the study of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungi"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Fungi"">fungi</a>, including their genetic and biochemical properties, their taxonomy and their use to humans as a source for tinder, medicine, food, and entheogens, as well as their dangers, such as poisoning or infection.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myofibril"" title=""Myofibril"">myofibril</a>"|"
A basic rod-like unit of a muscle <a href=""#cell""><span title=""See entry on this page at § cell"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">cell</span></a>.

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myosin"" title=""Myosin"">myosin</a>"|"

"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_selection"" title=""Natural selection"">natural selection</a>"|"
 A process in nature in which organisms possessing certain <a href=""#genotype""><span title=""See entry on this page at § genotype"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">genotypic</span></a> characteristics that make them better adjusted to an environment tend to survive, reproduce, increase in number or <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene_frequency"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Gene frequency"">frequency</a>, and therefore, are able to transmit and perpetuate their essential genotypic qualities to succeeding generations.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neurobiology"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Neurobiology"">neurobiology</a>"|"

The scientific study of the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nervous_system"" title=""Nervous system"">nervous system</a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuron"" title=""Neuron"">neuron</a>"|"

"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neurotransmitter"" title=""Neurotransmitter"">neurotransmitter</a>"|"
An <a href=""#endogenous""><span title=""See entry on this page at § endogenous"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">endogenous</span></a> compound that enable <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neurotransmission"" title=""Neurotransmission"">neurotransmission</a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_niche"" title=""Ecological niche"">niche</a>"|"
The role and position an organism or taxon fills within its environment; how it meets its needs for food and shelter, how it survives, and how it reproduces. A species' niche includes all of its interactions with the biotic and abiotic factors of its environment.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleic_acid"" title=""Nucleic acid"">nucleic acid</a>"|"
The biopolymers, or small <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomolecules"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Biomolecules"">biomolecules</a>, essential to all known forms of life .
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleic_acid_sequence"" title=""Nucleic acid sequence"">nucleic acid sequence</a>"|"
 A succession of letters that indicate the order of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleotides"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Nucleotides"">nucleotides</a> forming alleles within a <a href=""#dNA""><span title=""See entry on this page at § DNA"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">DNA</span></a> or <a href=""#rNA""><span title=""See entry on this page at § RNA"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">RNA</span></a> molecule.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleobase"" title=""Nucleobase"">nucleobase</a>"|"
The nitrogen-containing biological compounds that form <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleosides"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Nucleosides"">nucleosides</a>, which in turn are components of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleotides"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Nucleotides"">nucleotides</a>, with all of these monomers constituting the basic building blocks of nucleic acids.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleoid"" title=""Nucleoid"">nucleoid</a>"|"

"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleolus"" title=""Nucleolus"">nucleolus</a>"|"
The largest structure within the <a href=""#nucleus""><span title=""See entry on this page at § nucleus"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">nucleus</span></a> of <a href=""#eukaryote""><span title=""See entry on this page at § eukaryote"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">eukaryotic</span></a> cells.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleotide"" title=""Nucleotide"">nucleotide</a>"|"
An organic compound which serves as the fundamental <a href=""#monomer""><span title=""See entry on this page at § monomer"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">monomer</span></a> used in the construction of <a href=""#nucleic_acid""><span title=""See entry on this page at § nucleic acid"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">nucleic acid</span></a> polymers, such as <a href=""#dNA""><span title=""See entry on this page at § DNA"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">DNA</span></a> and <a href=""#rNA""><span title=""See entry on this page at § RNA"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">RNA</span></a>, both of which are essential biomolecules within all living organisms.

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcription_(genetics)"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Transcription (genetics)"">transcription</a>"|"
Transcription is the first step of gene expression, in which a particular segment of DNA is copied into RNA (mRNA) by the enzyme RNA polymerase. Both RNA and DNA are nucleic acids, which use base pairs of nucleotides as a complementary language.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(biology)"" title=""Translation (biology)"">translation</a>"|"
The process in which <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ribosomes"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Ribosomes"">ribosomes</a> in the cytoplasm or ER synthesize proteins after the process of transcription of <a href=""#dNA""><span title=""See entry on this page at § DNA"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">DNA</span></a> to <a href=""#rNA""><span title=""See entry on this page at § RNA"" class=""glossary-link-internal"" style=""border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;"" id="""">RNA</span></a> in the cell's nucleus.

"

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uterus"" title=""Uterus"">uterus</a>"|"

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuole"" title=""Vacuole"">vacuole</a>"|"

"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valence_electron"" title=""Valence electron"">valence electron</a>"|"
A valence electron is an electron that is associated with an atom, and that can participate in the formation of a chemical bond; in a single covalent bond, both atoms in the bond contribute one valence electron in order to form a shared pair.

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zooplankton"" title=""Zooplankton"">zooplankton</a>"|"
Are <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heterotroph"" title=""Heterotroph"">heterotrophic</a> (sometimes <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detritivore"" title=""Detritivore"">detritivorous</a>) <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plankton"" title=""Plankton"">plankton</a> (cf. <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phytoplankton"" title=""Phytoplankton"">phytoplankton</a>). Plankton are organisms drifting in <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean"" title=""Ocean"">oceans</a>, <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea"" title=""Sea"">seas</a>, and bodies of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fresh_water"" title=""Fresh water"">fresh water</a>. Individual zooplankton are usually <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microscopic_scale"" title=""Microscopic scale"">microscopic</a>, but some (such as <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jellyfish"" title=""Jellyfish"">jellyfish</a>) are larger and <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macroscopic_scale"" title=""Macroscopic scale"">visible</a> to the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naked_eye"" title=""Naked eye"">naked eye</a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zygospore"" title=""Zygospore"">zygospore</a>"|"

"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zygote"" title=""Zygote"">zygote</a>"|"
A <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryotic"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Eukaryotic"">eukaryotic</a> cell formed by a fertilization event between two <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gametes"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Gametes"">gametes</a>.
